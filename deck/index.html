What this plan had to solve

Three challenges shaped
this plan.

The same three you'll be working with at your tables. Each pairs a familiar trap with the move the team made instead.

Challenge 01

Servicing at the periphery.

Bring water, sanitary, and storm to a site at the edge of the service envelope — without overbuilding for demand that hasn't arrived.

Challenge 02

Public land as a policy lever.

65% of the plan area is City-owned. 100% of Phases 1 & 2. The lever only works if disposition is deliberate.

Challenge 03

A complete community in isolation.

No neighbouring village to graft onto. Whatever opens day one has to be self-sufficient — and incremental enough not to fail.

Challenge 1 of 3

01

Servicing at the periphery.

The plan area sits outside the existing service envelope — no municipal water, sanitary, or stormwater. A new booster station, a storage tank, and lift station upgrades are required before anyone occupies a unit. EXP's initial feasibility study told us whether services could even reach the site — and at what cost. It set the frame for everything else.

The trap

Spend big upfront.

Front-load all servicing to enable maximum buildout — and end up with empty pipes for a decade if absorption is slow.

+

Our move

Phase the pipes with the plan.

Feasibility study, urban-design layout, and densities sized together. Servicing sequenced phase-by-phase — Phase 1 infrastructure for Phase 1 buildout, with deliberate triggers for the next round.

~90 seconds. The engineering challenge — keep it concrete. Set up the three-challenges frame here so it carries through the next two slides and into the workshop. Sample line: "Three challenges. They're the three this project asked us to figure out, and they're the same three I want you wrestling with when you get to your tables — because they don't disappear once a plan is adopted. First: servicing. The plan area is at the edge. No water, no sanitary, no storm. Bringing services means a new booster station, a new storage tank, lift station upgrades on Wilsey Road, and culverts. Big capital. The trap is obvious — overbuild upfront, hope demand catches up, and you end up with stranded municipal assets. What EXP did instead was an initial feasibility study right at the front of the project to figure out what services could actually reach the site, and at what cost. That study set the ceiling. Then the land-use framework and the densities were sized inside that ceiling, with the phasing matched to the servicing increments. Slower, but the dollars match the demand." Pause on the "+". It's the through-line.

Challenge 2 of 3

02

Public land as a policy lever.

The City owns 65% of the plan area overall — and 100% of Phases 1 and 2. That's a rare degree of control over form, affordability, and pace at exactly the moment tone is being set. But the lever only works if disposition is deliberate.

The trap

Sell the land, write the plan.

Treat public land like any other parcel — sell to maximize revenue, lose the affordability and form-control upside the plan is supposed to deliver.

+

Our move

Tie release to phase triggers.

Use the secondary plan to direct land disposition: parcels release on phase milestones with conditions on housing mix, frontage, and density baked into the disposal terms.

~90 seconds. This is the political/policy challenge. Sample line: "Second challenge — public land. Sixty-five percent across the plan area. And — this is the part to hold onto — one hundred percent of the first two phases. The phases you are about to work on at your tables are entirely on City land. That is a lot of leverage at exactly the moment that the tone for the whole node is set. But it only works if you treat it as a planning instrument, not a revenue source. The trap is to write a beautiful secondary plan, then sell parcels for top dollar to whoever shows up, and watch the affordability targets evaporate at land disposal. The strategy was to use the secondary plan to direct disposition: phase triggers tied to land release, conditions on housing mix and form embedded in the sale terms. The plan and the parcels move together." This is also the slide where the audience is most likely to ask questions later. Be ready.

Challenge 3 of 3

03

A complete community in isolation.

The site has no village to graft onto. No corner store, no school, no transit. Whatever opens on day one has to be self-sufficient enough to function alone — and incremental enough to not collapse waiting for demand to catch up. Get this wrong at the start and you've designed a car-centric neighbourhood that's almost impossible to retrofit later.

The trap

Build the centre first.

Open the mixed-use Central Node early to "set the tone" — and watch ground-floor commercial sit empty for years while the residential population it depends on slowly arrives.

+

Our move

Activate the node incrementally, anchored to the corridors.

Sequence the Central Node with Corridor frontage on Alison Boulevard and Vanier so the community has a functioning main street from Phase 1. Commercial scales up on routes people already drive — the village centre fills in as the village arrives.

~90 seconds. The urbanism challenge — the one most planners in the room will recognize. Sample line: "Third — and this is the one that's going to come back at you in the workshop. The site is isolated. There's no neighbouring village to lean on. So whatever we open in Phase 1 has to be a complete community on its own — somewhere people can actually live, walk to a store, send a kid to school. That's the bar. The plan calls for a mixed-use Central Node to anchor that — village centre, shops, residential above. The problem with central nodes is they need critical mass, and residential demand builds slowly. So you can't open Phase 1 with a hollow town square waiting for customers. Our move was to spread activation across the two Corridors — Alison and Vanier — alongside the Central Node. Those are routes that already carry traffic. Commercial can activate incrementally there while the Central Node fills in over time. The community gets a functioning main street from day one." This sets up the workshop directly. Land it.

The answer the plan landed on

Six phases. Three logics.

Phases 1 & 2 — open the corridor edge against existing services and access. Unlock the Central Node early so the neighbourhood has a working main street from the start.

Phases 3 & 4 — infill behind demonstrated demand and the major servicing investment that comes with it. Extend connections to Alison Boulevard for future private-land phases.

Phases 5 & 6 — continue infill to support the Central Node as the area builds out.

Hatched area, southeast: reserved as a potential provincial public-school site — fields, gym, basketball courts as part of the same investment.

You're about to look at how Phases 1 and 2 build out.

Updated phasing plan — Southeast Secondary Plan, Jan 2026.

~60 seconds. Show, don't read. Let the map speak. Sample line: "Here's where the plan landed. Six phases, grouped into three logics. The arrangement isn't accidental — it tracks the three challenges I just walked through. Phases one and two together thread the corridor where access and servicing already exist, and they unlock the Central Node early so the neighbourhood has a working main street from the start. Phases three and four infill behind that — they're the phases that come on the back of demonstrated demand and the major servicing investment that goes with them, and they extend connections back up to Alison Boulevard for future phases on private lands. Phases five and six continue the infill around the Central Node. One thing worth flagging — the hatched area in the southeast is reserved as a potential provincial public-school site. If the Province moves on it, a school here would integrate the community amenities this neighbourhood needs anyway — playing fields, gym, basketball courts — into a single capital investment. That's a partnership lever the plan is deliberately leaving room for. In about three minutes, you're going to be looking at this map at your tables, focused on phases one and two." At your tables — prompts to keep open

Five questions to anchor the conversation.

You don't have to answer all of them. They're here to keep the conversation honest — and to help unstick a table that's circling.

  1. 01

    Phase 1, day one.

    Stand at the edge of Phase 1 on the morning of move-in. What does a resident need within a five-minute walk for this to feel like a neighbourhood and not a subdivision?

  2. 02

    Scale that pencils.

    The Central Node is in Phase 2. What's the smallest viable version of it that can open without sitting empty waiting for residential to catch up?

  3. 03

    Mix without sprawl.

    Phase 1 is residential plus "some other uses." What other uses, where, and at what frontage — without diluting the Central Node before it opens?

  4. 04

    Walkable from isolation.

    The site has no neighbouring village. Which streets, frontages, or block sizes in Phase 1 and 2 do the most work to discourage car-only living before the rest of the node arrives?

  5. 05

    The trigger.

    What has to be true on the ground for it to be the right moment to move from Phase 1 into Phase 2? Population? Absorption? Servicing milestone? Something else?

NOT a slide you speak to. Click into it as you wrap, then walk to the tables. The slide stays on screen for the duration of the workshop so anyone who looks up sees the prompts. If anyone asks "do we answer all five?" — no. They're scaffolding, not a checklist.
